The Origin of the Bros

April 2026 | By Kyle Fournier

I met Steve in the fall of 2004 when I joined Coventry High School’s Cross-Country team. He was rude, obnoxious, mischievous - and vibrant. We got along immediately. I had no way of knowing - of course - that girl talk and dick jokes would change the entire course of my life.

If you’re not familiar with Cross-Country (XC, for short), it’s this very stupid “sport” in which you train by running a lot and then you compete by running a lot. Sorry XC enthusiasts, that’s just how it is.

The Weight of the Run

I had already ran Indoor Track the previous Winter and Spring for one miserable season. In truth, I had no passion for running. I had been dealing with weight problems and body dysmorphia ever since I could remember and after watching Rocky IV, I decided to start running over the summer break in between Freshman and Sophomore year. Along with a borderline anorexic diet and the magic of puberty, running daily helped me thin out dramatically. It delighted me when people (read: girls) were shocked at how different I looked when school started again.

The rewards of running outweighed its pain and monotony, so I kept at it. Then, it paid off during gym class. For some sadistic reason, gym teachers like to do “physical fitness” tests once a year where everyone is forced to do the things cadets are forced to do in the police academy: Pushups, Situps, Pullups, and the 1 mile run. As a fat kid my entire life, I dreaded them. After months of training for an event that I never even considered, however, I cherished the opportunity to run some laps.

I sucked at everything else, but I put up a decent enough time in the mile. Decent enough that a senior whom I looked up to told me I should try running as a sport. It was a ridiculous idea but that guy was cool, smart, and super handsome, so I basically did it just because he encouraged me too.

And so I ran track.

Finding My Guys

Track SUCKED. It began when Winter began, so we ran in the bitter, freezing, dick-shriveling cold. That’s not even a joke. When it hit the negative Farenheits, it was inevitable that someone would bring up the fact that they found something in their tights that felt more like an ice cube than a penis. But I digress.Where was I? Oh yeah.

Track SUCKED. Running in the cold sucked. Running in the gym when it was too cold to run outside sucked. The coach sucked. Yeah, maybe that’s most of it actually. The coach was too serious, too mean, and too racist. Seriously. Nobody had fun.I was ready to hang up my track spikes for good when that same handsome senior implored me to try XC. I still idolized him to some degree, so once again, I took his word for it.

He was right. The bitter cold of running in winter was replaced by the pleasant transition of summer to winter. The intense speedwork was replaced by longer, slower miles. The douchebag coach was replaced by Mr. Warner, a guy I’ll always remember as wise, kind, fatherly, and tolerant.

And believe you me, he was tolerant. Whereas we ran in a state of quiet discontent during Track, we were having a blast every single day when we showed up to XC. Seriously, I’m not exaggerating. It sounds impossible, but I looked forward to finishing school, throwing on the absolutely putrid shorts and shirt that I never washed, and heading out to run eight miles with the guys. Because they were becoming my guys. Fast.

When I ran Track, I ran one of the longer events - the 1500m, which is just shy of one mile. Despite running the second longest race, our workouts were rarely slow .They most often had us running brutally fast (well, not my slow ass) repeats of shorter distances. There was no time to have fun or connect with my teammates.

As Mr. Warner said, training for Cross-Country races necessitated “long, slow miles” at a “conversational” pace. For the first time in my life, I was running in a group of guys that actually talked with one another. And for the first time in my life, I was experiencing that special sort of bond that can only be fostered through endurance and struggle. Yes, Cross-Country training was less intense - but don’t get me wrong, training seriously for races inevitably brings a certain type of pain that non-runners will never know. That pain, when shared, yields respect. And while most of our time was spent making extremely immature, politically incorrect jokes and bullying each other, the respect cemented lifelong friendships.

The Truth within the Mischief

And finally, we reach Steve. I have kept in touch and even stayed good friends with several of the guys from Cross-Country back in 2004, but Steve and I built something that will last a lifetime - of that, I have no doubt.

Steve was my polar (unintended, I swear) opposite. While I had developed my own streak of irreverence in the latter years of high school, I was - by all accounts - a “good” kid. I got excellent grades, I was polite to my teachers, and the first time I was officially disciplined came when I was 15 years old. All throughout my adolescence, I behaved well. Steve… did not. In fact, he was one of the worst-behaved kids I knew.

I can’t remember a single day during which Steve wasn’t involved in some sort of shenanigans, tomfoolery, or general rapscallionism. To every single adult who worked at Coventry High School, he was a terror. Although I believe he’s safe due to the statute of limitations, I’m not sure I can even tell you some of the things he did. But that’s probably why I was so drawn to him.

More than anything else, Steve was himself. He was - and still is - the most authentic person in any setting. He made trouble because he wanted to make trouble. I had always wanted to make trouble, but I was too afraid of some dark, unknown consequences that Steve always seemed to elude. In fact, Steve’s behavior seemed to be a net positive for him and his life.

While he was always in some sort of trouble and annoying at least one teacher or coach, everyone loved him. His behavior was never (well… rarely) mean-spirited. It was all in the name of fun. He made almost everyone laugh. And the few who wouldn’t laugh were the type who wouldn’t laugh at anything.

Steve’s antics were what drew me to him, but it was something else that led to us developing the deepest type of friendship there is. In Steve, I saw something strange. Something different. Something I didn’t yet have the words for. And while Steve and I were polar opposites, when I saw Steve, I saw myself.

The Turning Point

During those long, slow miles, the team ran together most of the time. But not everyone runs at the same pace. So, inevitably, when the faster kids surged ahead and slower kids fell behind, smaller groups, trios, and duos stuck together. Of course, there were the over-eager solos at the front and the pitiful solos at the back, too, but that’s neither here nor there.

Steve was fast and I was slow, but he must have seen the same thing in me as I saw in him, because he started matching my pace to spend more time talking with me one-on-one. Mr Warner didn’t like that, but sometimes Mr. Warner wasn’t fast enough to catch up and do anything about it,

Steve’s openness inspired me to open up, too. We talked about school, our parents, video games, girls, and who knows what else. Really, that list is woefully inadequate. Because we talked about *everything*.

I have a vivid memory of the first time Steve and I realized we were the same person; it was when we broached the subject of body dysmorphia - another thing we felt and could describe but didn’t have the knowledge to name with two medical words.

The day’s run - a quicker than normal 5 miles - had just started. I followed Steve’s lead that day and pushed ahead with him. We did that sometimes. When I inevitably died, we’d go from an all-out sprint to a brisk jog until I caught my breath. Anyway, we were rounding the corner of ClubHouse Road and entering Wood Estates. Steve’s energy was calmer than usual, almost somber. He said, “I feel fat.” Already winded, I barely managed, “What?”

Steve had been running daily for several years at that point. He was one of the top three fastest runners on our team. And even a high-schooler’s trash diet couldn’t outpace that mileage. He was six feet tall and maybe 140lbs on a day when he had eaten an unusually high number of items from Taco Bell’s dollar menu. Unless you asked someone who had a reason to make Steve feel bad, you could not possibly find a single person in the world who would describe Steve as “fat.”

But I felt the same. The exact same. My earliest memories of events in my life are paired with shame and guilt for being “fat.” I spent my entire childhood self-conscious because I was fat. Then, when I lost weight and started running, I wasn’t fat anymore - but I still felt fat. It was a private feeling. A private thought. But Steve knew it. Steve lived it. And I couldn’t believe it. We were the same.

When Steve’s words and feelings sunk in and I replied, “Me too,” I saw him change. Not his face - we ran side by side. I saw it first in his stride. His swinging arms lost their impeccable running form and loosened. His steps landed on the pavement with less force. We slowed down without saying a word.

The second pack of runners passed us. Then the third. Then Mr. Warner was behind us, yelling at us. But really he was yelling at Steve. Steve had no right to be running anywhere near Warner’s pace. Warner knew I was the reason Steve slowed down, but Steve was the captain. It was Steve’s responsibility to run fast.

So we picked it up. We found a nice quiet spot between the second fastest group and the third fastest group. The vanguard - the fastest kid on our team - was a machine with no need for friends. For the purposes of this story, he can be seen as some sort of ethereal being, an entity that soared above and beyond any of the humans I’m writing about.

That nice, quiet pocket allowed us more space to talk. And with a reasonable pace, I could actually contribute to the conversation. So we talked it out.

We talked about the mirror. The fucking mirror. God, I hated that mirror. And Steve did too! I can’t adequately describe the feeling I had when Steve described my own inner monologues, so I won’t try. But I can tell you in one sentence what Steve did for me. He did something that I’ve only found in my favorite books. He made me feel less alone.

In each other, we found ourselves. From that day forward, we continued joking around, pranking people, and making each other laugh by saying the absolute most heinous things we could think of. But all of those jokes, all of those laughs - they acted as the foundation for something deeper. In the midst of all of our fun, we built a safe space for each other. We allowed each other to say everything we never dared say to anyone else.

And that’s what made Steve and I bros.
