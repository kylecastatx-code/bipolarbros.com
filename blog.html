Don't Worry, Take Your Time

April 2026 | By Stephen Griffith

It all led to this.

Bipolar 2, specifically, is characterized by long, grueling bouts of depression interspersed with far shorter bursts of hypomania and periods of what most people (I assume) would describe as “normal” moods.

My depressions are fucking long. They’ve lasted anywhere from six months to - more often - roughly two years. I don’t experience the rapid changes that people with Bipolar 1 do. I’m discussing all of this to make a point: I’ve gone through torturously long phases during which I’ve created absolutely nothing.

The book Touched with Fire by Kay Redfield Jamieson was revelatory for me. If you’re creative in any sense (most people with Bipolar are), I implore you to read it. The book examines and dissects the link between creative genius and Bipolar Disorder and, accordingly, the correlation of productivity with moods. It explained that my utter lack of productivity during depressive periods is not uncommon. Far from it.

In fact, most of the great artists you know, whether they be painters, writers, musicians, or otherwise, likely suffered from Bipolar Disorder. And many of them go through long periods during which they simply cannot create anything.

My inability to work or create in any way for such long stretches of time is the source of (maybe) my greatest guilt and shame. As a bright child, I was told that I had “so much potential.” Great. What’s not great, however, is when you fail to live up to it.

School was good for me. It forced me to study and create through necessity and deadlines. When my teachers and parents demanded that I be productive, I could do it. When I claimed autonomy, though, my drive to do anything became entirely dependent on how I was feeling at any given time.

I don’t give a fuck about money. I never have. What I want is freedom and space to create. I still barely know what that means. Am I a writer, a speaker, or something else? I’m not sure. This indecisiveness coupled with depression’s constriction of my energy has seen me totally paralyzed for most of my adult life. And to be completely honest with you, I hate myself for it.

Luckily, age, experience, and wise friends have taught me that I can and should accept this aspect of myself. Not only that, I’ve learned that this self-recrimination is false - as all self-recriminations are.

While I haven’t accomplished my dream of publishing books or growing a Podcast audience, I have done things. I’ve written a lot. I’ve recorded podcasts. I’ve studied various topics. And it all led to this.

If you’re reading this and you suck, you probably don’t. You’ve found the Bipolar Bros and that means you're a curious person with the desire to learn more about yourself and improve yourself in whatever ways you can. You’ve been on your own unique paths, been beaten down by your own unique struggles and calamities, and gained your own unique insights.

Try as I may, I don’t fit into any clear boxes - like “writer” or “speaker” or otherwise. And you probably don’t either. But that’s a good thing. The totality of your life has led you to this. It’s given you a wealth of experiences, ideas, and skills that nobody else in the world has. Maybe you haven’t figured out what to do with it yet, but you will. I have faith in you.
